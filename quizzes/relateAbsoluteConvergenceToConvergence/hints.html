We are told that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n|\) converges, which means that the series is absolutely convergent.

Since \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n|\) converges, so too does \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty 2|a_n|\).

But note that \(0 \leq a_n+|a_n| \leq 2|a_n|\).

Consequently, by the comparison test, the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \left( a_n + |a_n| \right)\) converges, since it has nonnegative terms and is smaller termwise than the convergent series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty 2|a_n|\).

Consider \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_n+|a_n|) -\sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n|\).

This is a difference of convergent series.

So \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_n+|a_n|) -\sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n| = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left( a_n+|a_n|-|a_n| \right)\) converges.

But that is just to say that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \left( a_n+|a_n|-|a_n| \right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \) converges.

So we conclude that the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \) converges.

This is usually stated as a theorem: absolute convergence implies convergence.
