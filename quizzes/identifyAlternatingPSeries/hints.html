Set \(a_{#{variable}} = #{latex(term)}\).

Note that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \neq 0\).

Note that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0\).

So the limit test is inconclusive in this case.

Therefore, the series diverges.

Let's check absolute convergence.

We then consider \(\sum_{#{variable} = #{initial}} \left| (-1)^#{variable}/#{variable}^{#{latex(p)}} \right|\) which is the \(p\)-series \(\sum_{#{variable} = #{initial}} 1/#{variable}^{#{latex(p)}}\).

Since \(p = #{latex(p)} > 1\), we may conclude that the series conveges absolutely.

Since \(p = #{latex(p)} < 1\), we may conclude that the series does not convege absolutely.

We should check for conditional convergence.

Note that \(\sum_{#{variable} = #{initial}} (-1)^#{variable}/#{variable}^{#{latex(p)}} \) is an alternating series.

Since \(|a_{#{variable}+1}| < |a_{#{variable}}|\), the sequence of absolute values is decreasing.

Note that \(\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0\).

Therefore, by the alternating series test, the series \(\sum_{#{variable} = #{initial}} (-1)^#{variable}/#{variable}^{#{latex(p)}} \) converges.

Since the series converges but not absolutely, we say that the series converges conditionally.
