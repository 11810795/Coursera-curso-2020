The first thing we should do when confronted with an unknown series is check the limit of the terms.

Recall that if the series converges, then the limit must exist and be equal to zero.

So if the limit does not exist or is not zero, then the series diverges.

In this case, we consider \(\displaystyle\lim_{#{variable} \to \infty} #{latex(term)}\).

Let's multiply by a “disguised version of one,” meaning \(\displaystyle\lim_{#{variable} \to \infty} #{latex(term)} \cdot \frac{1/#{latex(variable**2)}}{1/#{latex(variable**2)}}\).

Expanding the numerator and denominator yields \(\displaystyle\lim_{#{variable} \to \infty} \frac{#{latex(term.numerator().expand()/variable**2)}}{#{latex(term.denominator().expand()/variable**2)}}\).

Dividing through by \(#{latex(variable**2)}\) gives \(\displaystyle\lim_{#{variable} \to \infty} \frac{#{latex((term.numerator().expand()/variable**2).expand())}}{#{latex((term.denominator().expand()/variable**2).expand())}}\).

Note then that \(\displaystyle\lim_{#{variable} \to \infty} \frac{#{latex((term.numerator().expand()/variable**2).expand())}}{#{latex((term.denominator().expand()/variable**2).expand())}} = #{latex(the_limit)}\).

But \(#{latex(the_limit)} \neq 0\), and so, this series must diverge.
