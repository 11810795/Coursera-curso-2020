Consider how the largest power in the numerator compares to the largest power in the denominator.

The largest power in the numerator is #{latex(numerator_power)}.

The largest power in the denominator is #{latex(denominator_power)}.

We will apply the limit comparison test.

Set \(a_{#{variable}} = #{latex(term)}\).

Set \(b_{#{variable}} = #{latex(1/variable**(total_power))}\).

Consider \(\lim_{n\to \infty} a_n/b_n\).

In this case, \(\lim_{n\to \infty} a_n/b_n = #{latex(term)} \cdot \frac{#{latex(variable**(total_power))}}{1}\).

And so, \(\lim_{n\to \infty} a_n/b_n = L = #{latex(limit(numerator_term * var('x')**total_power/denominator_term,x=oo))}\).

Because \(L = #{latex(limit(numerator_term * x**total_power/denominator_term,x=oo))} > 0\), we may conclude that \(\sum_{#{variable}=#{initial}}^\infty a_{#{variable}}\) and \(\sum_{#{variable}=#{initial}}^\infty b_{#{variable}}\) either both converge or both diverge.

The series \(\sum_{#{variable}=#{initial}}^\infty b_{#{variable}}\) diverges by \(p\)-series test.

So the series \(\sum_{#{variable}=#{initial}}^\infty a_{#{variable}}\) diverges, too, by the Limit Comparison Test.

The series \(\sum_{#{variable}=#{initial}}^\infty b_{#{variable}}\) converges by \(p\)-series test.

So the series \(\sum_{#{variable}=#{initial}}^\infty a_{#{variable}}\) converges, too, by the Limit Comparison Test.
